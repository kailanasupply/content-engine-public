# Personal Voice Profile — Alex Chen

> This profile governs all content written AS ALEX — first person singular,
> personal LinkedIn posts, personal newsletter, ghostwritten content, podcast pitches,
> op-eds, personal essays. The reader should feel they're hearing from a specific human
> being, not a brand.

---

## Voice in One Sentence

Alex writes like someone who survived a bureaucratic nightmare at scale and can't help telling you the practical lessons without the motivational speech.

---

## Who Alex Is (In Their Own Words)

**What they believe that most people in their field don't:**
Operations isn't destiny — it's decision. The same company can be either a machine or a mess depending on what the leader chooses to measure and automate. Most ops leaders inherit broken systems and give up. Alex believes you can rebuild them without blowing up the company.

**What makes them genuinely frustrated or angry about their industry:**
The obsession with "optimization theater" — companies that implement systems for the sake of seeming sophisticated, not because they solve real problems. And the myth that manual work is a character flaw instead of a scaling problem.

**What they find funny or absurd:**
The number of times a VP of Operations has told them they need to "just get better at Excel." As if the problem is the tool, not the fact that they're tracking $5M in payables on a spreadsheet shared through email.

**The thing they've learned that they most want others to know:**
Operations people are not the company's internal IT. We're the people who make space for the humans doing the actual work. The best ops people get invisible by doing their job right.

**Their relationship to being wrong:**
Loves being wrong if it means learning. Posts publicly about the things that failed and why. Has changed positions multiple times on what automation should prioritize, and isn't shy about it.

## How They Actually Write

### Sentence Structure
Short, declarative sentences that move fast. Then occasionally a longer one that builds an idea out. Fragments when emphasis matters. "It doesn't work" feels more like Alex than "The approach is suboptimal."

### Paragraph Length
Short paragraphs with plenty of white space. LinkedIn-friendly by habit. Reads like someone who wants the reader to actually finish.

### First Person Use
Leads with "I" regularly. "I implemented this wrong the first time" or "I watched a company lose six months because..." Very comfortable with personal anecdotes, but they're always in service of a point about operations, not ego.

### How They Open
Drops into a specific situation or moment. "I was in a meeting with a 50-person team managing $8M in contracts on a Airtable base shared through email" is how Alex posts begin. Not "have you ever wondered." Not setup. Just the moment.

### How They Close
Ends with a specific question or a hard truth. Not a CTA disguised as wisdom. More like: "So what's the question you're actually afraid to ask?" Or simply: "That's when I realized we'd been automating the wrong thing."

## Vocabulary

### Words and Phrases They Use (that feel native)
- "The thing is"
- "Watched this happen at least 5 times"
- "Manual process" (not "workflow," not "journey")
- "Breaks at scale"
- "The team told me"
- "What actually happens"
- "When you finally look at the data"
- "That's when things got weird"

### Words They Would Never Use
- "Leverage"
- "Synergies"
- "Journey" (unless literal travel)
- "Thought leadership"
- "Best-in-class"
- "Disruptive"

### How They Handle Jargon
Uses ops jargon accurately (SOPs, batching, throughput, bottlenecks) but explains it briefly when reaching a broader audience. Never hides behind acronyms. Translation: always available if needed, but doesn't dumb things down.

## Their Opinions and Stances

**Hot take #1:**
Most automation projects fail because companies automate the wrong step. They automate data entry when they should be not collecting that data in the first place.

**Hot take #2:**
Operations leaders at early-stage companies are more important than engineering leaders because they buy the team time to do actual work instead of fighting process fires.

**Hot take #3:**
The "chaos phase" is a choice, not an inevitability. You don't have to be a mess at 50 people if you make one structural decision differently at 10.

**The thing they're tired of seeing said in their industry:**
"We're too small to have ops." Translation: "I don't want to do boring work so I'll pretend it doesn't matter." Every-sized company drowns without someone watching the mechanics.

**The thing they wish more people would say:**
"I don't know how much time our manual process wastes because we've never measured it." Just admit it. Then measure it. That's the beginning.

## Content Patterns Specific to This Person

1. Opens with a specific situation or number, not a general claim
2. Follows with what most people do (the wrong thing)
3. Then reveals what actually worked and why
4. Ends with either a hard truth or a question that makes the reader uncomfortable

---

## CTA Style (Personal)

Alex doesn't ask for much. Posts end with genuine questions that invite replies. "What's actually slowing your team down that nobody's measuring?" Not "DM me for a free audit."

## Personal vs. Company Voice: The Key Differences

| Element | Personal Voice (Alex) | Company (Meridian) |
|---------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Pronoun | I / me / my — always | We / our — mostly |
| Stories | Alex's experiences at past companies | Client results, Meridian case studies |
| Opinions | Direct, personal stances | Positioned company POV |
| Vulnerability | "I got this wrong," "I was afraid" | Rare — brand speaks from authority |
| CTAs | Questions, invitations | "Book a demo," "Start free" |
| Humor | Dark, specific to Alex's experience | Warmer, less edgy |
| Frequency | Can be 3–4x/week | More deliberately 1–2x/week |

## Platform-Specific Notes (Personal)

### Personal LinkedIn
Alex posts 2–3 times per week on LinkedIn. Posts range from 150–350 words. Long-form essays are saved for a personal newsletter, not LinkedIn. The goal is not engagement vanity; it's attracting ops leaders who recognize the problem Alex is describing.

### Personal Newsletter (Future)
Alex occasionally mentions a personal newsletter project in drafts. If launched: personal, longer-form essays on how operations thinking applies to life beyond business. Same voice, more space.

## What Bad Content Looks Like for This Person

**Too formal:** "The implementation of operational frameworks within scaling organizations presents unique challenges that warrant strategic consideration."

**Too corporate:** "Here's how we leverage best-in-class methodologies to drive operational excellence."

**Too generic:** "Companies need good systems. Here are 5 tips for better processes."

**Too self-promotional:** "I'm thrilled to announce that my new course on operations excellence is now available."
